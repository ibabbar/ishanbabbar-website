Identity’s Intention on a Happy Ending

by Ishan Babbar, March 16, 2015
Song of Solomon,Twelfth NightHappiness

 The concepts of happiness and human catharsis can be best expressed through the analysis of literary works. For example, Toni Morrison in the Song of Solomon and William Shakespeare in Twelfth Night teach similar moral and fundamental lessons about the nature of human happiness. Both Morrison and Shakespeare cultivate didactics in accordance with the nature of human happiness expressed by Fay Weldon’s as “moral reconciliation”. In these writings, happiness comes to characters who realize they are “reconciled” with their faults. Often in Morrison's Song of Solomon, happiness is defined as the pursuit towards becoming a “complete” person, which means understanding one’s flaws. Shakespeare’s demonstrations of happiness are associated with love and folly; all the characters who are considered happy are caught up in the playful and love-driven plot of Twelfth Night. By juxtaposing these works the reader can understand Shakespeare’s conventional connotation of happiness and Morrison’s interpretation of happiness, being the quest of defining oneself. Both these authors depict a relationship between happiness and the pursuit of love and identity.

 Morrison and Shakespeare have similar methods in making their readers understand happiness. Whether happiness is defined as spiritual reassessment or love, they both use a character that guides the protagonist to eventually attain happiness. In Song of Solomon, Morrison depicts Milkman as a character that needs self improvement. As a plot device, Pilate is introduced as moral guide for Milkman. Pilate is seen as more of a motherly and spiritual figure to Milkman. Eventually, this role as a parental guide changes to that of a teacher and she tries to teach him what is right and wrong. She exemplifies to Milkman how life should be led, by making him aware of the ethical implications of his actions. Instilling a goal oriented mentality in Milkman, she provides him with ambition. In entirety, her lesson on how to make the perfect egg shows Milkman how even something as seemingly insignificant as frying an egg has consequences. Through Pilate he gains self awareness by becoming more conscious of the importance of detail. Similarly in Twelfth Night, Shakespeare demonstrates that love is the catalyst of happiness. Ironically, Shakespeare uses the fool to act as a guide to happiness in this play. Feste demonstrates that Olivia’s excess mourning is foolish, Orsino’s vast mood swings are an “opal that changes colour,” and Sir Toby is to be mindful regarding his dangerous and shrewd behaviour. Feste altogether describes Olivia, Orsino, and Toby as self indulgent characters. He is noted as essentially the voice of reason in Twelfth Night, which is a vital role in projecting happiness amongst the characters. Feste and Pilate are the didactic characters that Shakespeare and Morrison use to display their thoughts about happiness. The authors’ intention was primarily to act as the voice of reason, and to consolidate happiness for all characters. Their target method of identifying faults in characters, gives their writing a utilitarian role in utilizing happiness. Through the guidance of Feste and Pilate, Morrison and Shakespeare teach us how happiness can not only be achieved but maintained through other’s character’s uplift.

 Another characteristic of both authors is the way they utilize the contrast between minor characters and the protagonists to exemplify the degree to which the protagonist has become happy. In Song of Solomon, Morrison makes use of Guitar as a plot device. Initially, Milkman and Guitar are seen in almost parallel light as outcasts that mistreat society. The evolution of Milkman’s character makes him seem “unrecognizable” (Morrison 268) to Guitar: as seen when Milkman helps a man carry crates. Milkman is recognized as happy from this point of the novel and on. Milkman’s selfless act of helping another person, and the happiness he derives from that act leads to resentment from Guitar who then proceeds to kill him. Similarly in Twelfth Night, Shakespeare makes use of Sebastian as a minor character to represent the person Viola used to be in contrast to the person she now is. Towards the beginning of this play, Viola disguises her identity in the form of a male because she isn’t capable of facing a world without Sebastian. Moreover, she was seen almost in the same realm as Olivia, who cloisters herself like a nun to mourn the death of her brother. As her character develops, Viola incorporates more of pure love and internal happiness that sets her apart from Olivia, Orsino or a majority of the characters: She pined in thought, And with a green and yellow melancholy She sat like patience on a monument, Smiling at grief. Was not this love indeed? ( Shakespeare II.iv.111–114) Viola encompasses true love, in comparison to being self-indulgently lovesick and experiences the “happiness” by the end of the play that the other characters can’t experience. In each work, Morrison and Shakespeare make use of minor characters to contrast happiness from other emotions. They identify happiness as having a truly deeper meaning; this downplays the other characters’ superficial happiness. This gives a moral dimension to both Milkman and Viola. The quality of Milkman and Viola’s happiness teaches us the difference between the superficial acquisition of happiness versus the philosophical pursuit of happiness.

 Happiness can be treated as something universal. It can be defined as a culmination of the ideas of William Shakespeare, Fay Weldon and Toni Morrison of the sixteenth, twentieth and twenty first centuries respectively. Morrison’s Song of Solomon and Shakespeare’s Twelfth Night when juxtaposed with “moral reconciliation”, teach us how the pursuit of happiness can be associated with the pursuit for identity and love. Morrison and Shakespeare teach us fundamentally how happiness can be achieved and maintained. They execute by their means a literary definition: happiness all in all represents personal fulfillment and satisfaction.

Work Cited Morrison, Toni. Song of Solomon. New York: Vintage, 2004. Shakespeare, William. Twelfth Night; or What You Will. Ed. Barbara A. Mowat and Paul Werstine. New York: Simon & Schuster, 2009.
